## The backdrop (assume this is true for the assignment)

- **Interest rates: higher-for-longer, but stabilizing.** Central banks in the US and Canada have paused. Rates are not expected to fall quickly. Assume the cost of borrowing stays elevated through the year.
- **Inflation:** cooling but still above target. No crisis, but no all-clear either.
- **Growth:** slow and uneven. No recession in the base case, but consumer spending is softening at the lower end.

## What this means by sector

- **High-multiple technology & semiconductors** — This group has run hard and now trades at rich valuations. It is the most **momentum-driven and rate-sensitive** corner of the market. A rotation *out* of crowded, expensive tech is a live risk this quarter. Exciting growth stories here can reverse quickly. Position sizing matters.
- **Highly leveraged, high-yield infrastructure (pipelines, some utilities)** — These names carry large debt loads by design and pay attractive dividends. In a higher-for-longer rate world, that **leverage and rate sensitivity is a risk, not just the yield being a reward.** A big dividend is not the same thing as a low-risk holding.
- **Large banks** — A higher-for-longer rate environment is generally a **tailwind** for net interest margins. Balance-sheet strength varies by name; the large, well-capitalized banks look relatively stable.
- **Quality consumer staples & defensive healthcare** — Lower-beta, steadier earnings. These tend to hold up when growth slows. Some quality staples trade at premium valuations, so you are paying up for that stability.
- **Energy producers & materials** — Commodity-price dependent and cyclical. Returns swing with oil, gas, and metals prices, which this note does not predict.
